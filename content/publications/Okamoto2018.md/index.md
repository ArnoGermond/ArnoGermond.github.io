abstract: In the stem cell research field, the molecular regulatory network used to define cellular states has been extensively studied, however, the general driving force guiding the collective state dynamics remains to be identified from biophysical aspects. Here we monitored the time-development of the cell-state transition at the single-cell and colony levels, simultaneously, during the early differentiation process in mouse embryonic stem cells. Our quantitative analyses revealed that cellular heterogeneity was a result of spontaneous fluctuation of cellular state and cell-cell cooperativity. We considered that the cell state is like a ball fluctuating on a potential landscape, and found that the cooperativity affects the fluctuation. Importantly, the cooperativity temporarily decreased and increased in the intermediate state of cell differentiation, leading to cell-state transition in unison. This process can be explained using the mathematical equation of flashing-ratchet behaviour, which suggests that a general mechanism is driving the collective decision-making of stem cells.
##
Note: Back in 2015, scientists working on stem cell differentiation observed that there is a strong gene expression heterogenity of the main transcription factors which drive differentiation. Studies mostly focused on bulk populations, or single cells. However, quantitative measurements done at the colony level were missing. Our working group started to investigate gene expression at the colony level and tested the following hypothesis is the cell-cell position (or cooperativity) a factor of differentiation? Our results on in silico models (based on Langevin equation) found that two major mechanisms are required to drive differentiation: the cell-to-cell spatial arrangements, and the variability of the cells's transcriptions factors. The model prediction matched with quantitative observations made on 100 colonies.
